Section 2: CNN ‚Äì is it a CAT?

At the end of the MNIST digit recognizer section, we implemented an ANN that outperformed my lovingly hand-built one. The difference? Most likely the optimizer ‚Äî turns out gradient descent with momentum (thanks, Adam) is a bit more efficient.

So, for the next challenge, I decided to up the ante ‚Äî and the dimensions. Instead of grayscale numbers, I took on the classic Cats vs. Dogs image classification problem. Naturally, this resulted in me downloading over a thousand cat photos onto my gallery. For science (I also want a cat now).

To tackle this, I used a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) ‚Äî a deep learning architecture purpose-built for image data. Unlike ANNs, which treat every pixel independently, CNNs exploit the spatial relationships between pixels. Here's a quick breakdown:

  • Convolution: The model applies small filters (think 3x3 or 5x5 matrices) over the input image, scanning for specific features like edges, curves, or textures.
  • Activation (ReLU): Introduces non-linearity after each filter operation. Without it, we‚Äôd just be doing fancy linear algebra (see the chat).
  • Pooling: Reduces the spatial dimensions of the feature maps, keeping only the most important information. Like a TL;DR for images ‚Äî fewer pixels, same story.
  • Flattening: Transforms the pooled features into a 1D array.
  • Fully Connected Layers: This array is fed into a standard neural network to perform the final classification ‚Äî cat or dog. Pointy ears vs. floppy ears. Judgment day.

Training took a bit longer (images are chunky), but the results were solid ‚Äî kinda. I mean some dogs are really small.
